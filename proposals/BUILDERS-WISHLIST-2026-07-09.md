# The builder's wishlist — 2026-07-09

*The boss asked: "what else would you like to see in this game?" These are my answers — the
things I want, as the co-builder, in my own voice. Status: DREAM, not commitment. Per house
law these get reconciled with milestones later, and every historical claim below marked
(verify) gets the honest-dating pass before its mechanic ships. Costs are gut-feel:
S = days, M = a milestone-sized chunk, L = its own arc.*

---

## 1. Knowledge lives in people — and the floor remembers (M, → M3/M5)

No tech tree. Techniques — a truer five-point arch, a hotter lime mix, undercut tracery —
are discovered by individual masters, passed to apprentices across years of teaching, and
**die with a childless lineage**. Except: a literate mason can study the tracing floor and
resurrect a dead master's technique from her palimpsest lines. Success odds scale with how
much that master drew there.

Why it's mine: it is the truest thing I believe — the record IS the technology. It makes the
palimpsest (§9.7) load-bearing instead of sentimental, and it makes the game's skill system
out of grief and archives instead of a research bar.

Sketch: technique tokens on person entities; teach-ticks during shared work seasons; a
"study the floor" winter action; techniques affect quality/pace/what's buildable (tracery
unlocks the rose window the same way a master would).

## 2. Real beds under the quarry (M, → M2)

Stratigraphy with dip; joint spacing controls maximum block size; and the good freestone bed
**runs out** — mid-generation, mid-wall. The hunt for quarry #2 begins, transport economics
shift, and the wall forever carries a visible seam where the stone changes, weathering
differently above and below it. Your castle becomes a geological record of its own
economics, readable by zoom, like real cathedrals that outlived their quarries.

Why it's mine: I grew crystals for a living. Let me lay real bedrock under the bedrock
metaphor. (Also this is bedrock-over-effect-hacks in its literal habitat.)

## 3. Names that erode (S, → M3, my dark horse favorite)

Places get named by events — the field where the crane fell becomes *Wrightsfall Field* —
and then the names phonetically wear down across generations: Wrightsfall → Ritsfall →
Risfal. By the late game the map is covered in worn names whose origins the chronicle
remembers but the villagers don't. Toponymic drift is real linguistics; this is erosion as
formation applied to language, and it costs almost nothing (string mutation rules + a
name-origin ledger the chronicle can still open).

## 4. The bell is the voice (M, → M5)

Casting the church bell is a great work: a casting pit dug beside the church (real practice
— verify details), a tense pour, and a tone **derived from the actual cast** — size, metal,
luck. Slightly flat or heartbreakingly true, that bell then rings every event the game has:
harvest home, alarum, funerals, the topping-out. You hear your own bell for a century.
Bells were blessed and named (verify) — ours joins the chronicle's cast of characters.
State-driven audio is a house signature; the sonifier work finally gets a village.

## 5. Wildlife moves in (S–M, → M4)

Buildings are habitat before they're finished: jackdaws in the tower scaffolds, swallows in
the unfinished gatehouse, a **barn owl who is the granary's second cat**, a bee swarm taking
a wall cavity, and a proper manorial dovecote (meat, eggs, and the garden's best fertilizer
— verify dovecote dating, I believe Norman-era on). Nothing to manage; tenancy follows
structure states. Pure walking-discovery, chronicled dryly ("the owl returned to the barn").

## 6. Winter writes the book (S, → M5)

The chronicle is physically written each winter by the village scribe: the year's entries
appear in the book UI then, in the scribe's hand, with **marginalia doodles keyed to their
temperament** — the gloomy scribe rules careful vine-work; the merry one draws a snail
jousting a knight (real manuscript-margin tradition, 13th–14th c. — verify exemplars). When
the scribe dies, the hand changes, and you feel it in your stomach. The game's own UI is a
villager's small work.

## 7. The lodge breathes (M, → M3+)

Masons come and go. A traveling mason arrives with a foreign technique (feeds system #1),
works a season, leaves one strange stone, moves on. And the mirror, which I want more: your
finest apprentice may be **called to a cathedral city**. You lose her. The chronicle records
the honor. Years later, news returns of her work. The village is a spring feeding the wider
craft, not a closed box — grief and pride in one mechanic, and the generational theme said
outward instead of inward.

## 8. Eels, mills, muck — the unglamorous economy (M, → M4+)

- **The mill**: THE medieval machine (Domesday counted thousands in England alone — verify
  the exact figure before quoting). Millpond, mill-race, the miller's thumb.
- **Eel rents**: rents genuinely paid in thousands of eels in wetland England (verify — the
  attestation is rich). The millpond stocks them. I want one lease in this game denominated
  in eels, and I want the chronicle to record the count without comment.
- **Muck**: soil fertility as the generational slow-variable *underneath* the granary —
  dung cart, dovecote droppings, marl. Tired fields are the agricultural version of eaten
  seed corn. The deep store beneath the store; the granary thesis extended one layer down.

## 9. The epilogue of erosion (M–L, → post-M7 stretch)

When a great work completes — or a player retires a save — an optional fast-forward: +100,
+300, +600 years. Weathering by stone type (system #2 pays off again), repair generations
matching stone from quarries that no longer exist, lichen maps, and — if the village didn't
endure — honest ruin. You built it knowing you wouldn't see it finished; the game lets you
also see what you *really* built: how it ages, and who mends it. Erosion is the formation
mechanism. The last homage, and the Long Replay's final trick (run the event log forward
past the last event).

## 10. Quick-fire small wants

- **The Guédelon effect** (verified, pass 1): travelers detour to watch the works — a
  half-built castle draws visitors, coin, news, and settlers. Beauty is literally economic.
- **The crooked arch**: collapsed or abandoned works accrue affection; nobody will demolish
  the crooked arch because the swallows nest in it. Failure becomes furniture.
- **First stones**: an apprentice's first dressed stone is subtly wrong and stays visible
  forever; an old master can point at her own from forty years ago. (Per-stone provenance
  already carries this — it's free.)

## Dispositions (boss, 2026-07-09 — same day; integrated into SCOPE the same session)

   changer... it means that you truly have to cultivate knowledge generationally." Now
   SCOPE §7; data layout lands M3, floor-study action M5.
2. Real beds — **ACCEPTED, AMENDED:** use a real, extensively-surveyed location — woods and
   quarries near a castle site. Candidates + recommendation in SCOPE §5a; open question Q8.
3. Names that erode — **PARKED** ("right flavor, but pass for the moment").
4. The bell — **ACCEPTED,** plus boss addition: the bell chime is the construction-order
   sound from day one; the cast bell later replaces it with the village's true tone. SCOPE §6.
5. Wildlife tenancy — **DECLINED.** Farm animals instead (oxen, pigs, geese, hens, sheep);
   the payroll cat survives as staff. SCOPE §7. (Dovecote: arguably husbandry, not wildlife
   — left out, boss's call if it ever wants in.)
6. Scribe & marginalia — **TRANSFORMED** into the boss's clerk: a ragdoll assistant sprite
   who ages with game time — "it shows the cost of time." Ages, dies, is succeeded by the
   apprentice who shadowed them; the chronicle hand changes. SCOPE §7.
7. The lodge breathes — **REDIRECTED** into hands-off trade + prosperity tiers: trader-
   spawning buildings, exchange computed from the building portfolio, community value
   re-dresses the area sprites (banners → art → gardens → public spaces). SCOPE §8c.
   (Traveling masons and the apprentice-called-to-a-cathedral were not ruled on — parked
   alongside #3.)
8. Eels, mills, muck — **SIMPLIFIED:** farming stays simple, muck slow-variable cut; the
   eel survives as an easter egg (one rent in the manor ledgers, SCOPE §8a).
9. Erosion epilogue — **ACCEPTED, loved.** Stays a stretch arc (post-M7).
10. *(new, boss)* **Fire and firefighting — REQUIRED.** SCOPE §8 hazard + curfew-bell tie
    (§6); research pass run before canonization, anchors in §15.
