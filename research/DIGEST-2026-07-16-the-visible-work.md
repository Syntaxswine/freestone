*Grounding pass for PROPOSAL-THE-VISIBLE-WORK-2026-07-16.md §3B′ (the skill system) and §3H (the
timber way). One web-verifying critic, fetch-checked citations, strengths marked honestly in the

## 1. The year rule — "a year of doing a job hardens a hand to it" — VERIFIED (in shape)

- **Navvy hardening (19th c., the direct quantified analogue):** "it took a year to turn a flabby
  labourer into a man hard enough" to hold a muck-gang shift (~20 tons/day) — Sullivan, *Navvyman*
  ch. 7 (victorianweb.org/history/work/sullivan/7.html; historylearningsite.co.uk navvies page).
  Caveat: 19th-century evidence, about conditioning + task skill, not a medieval record.
- **The year as the labor-market quantum:** early-modern English farm service was contracted BY THE
  YEAR (Kussmaul, *Servants in Husbandry in Early Modern England*, CUP; bahs.org.uk AGHR 48n2a1).
- **Craft apprenticeship sits safely ABOVE green:** guild apprenticeships ran 2–7 years (Statute of
  Artificers 1563 fixed seven; worldhistory.org Medieval Trades; brewminate.com guild overview), and
  Guédelon's quarrymen took years beyond basic competence to reach fluency. Mapping the boss's
  "basic" to the GREEN band, below journeyman/master, respects this.
- **Flavor phrasing that is exactly supported:** *"a year of the work hardens a hand to it."*

## 2. The green-band step (+12.5%) — DESIGN, NOT HISTORY (PARTLY)

- Medieval building accounts pay laborers ONE flat day rate — the record cannot quantify a
  within-laborer experience gap. What IS quantified is the craftsman-vs-laborer wage premium:
  ~100% before 1350, ~50% after 1400 (Clark, *The Long March of History* wage series, UC Davis).
- Adjacent evidence (navvy fresh-vs-hardened output; the craft premium) suggests real gaps were
  LARGER than 12.5% — the constant is a deliberately conservative floor inside the roadmap's canon
  ~15–25% band-spread cap, which itself deliberately compresses the historical premium so skill
  never becomes a shadow tech tree. **Never present the cap or the step as historically
  calibrated.**

## 3. In-game rates sanity-checked — VERIFIED (with one verb pinned)

- **Masons 24–36 stones LAID/day** = 0.81–1.22 m³/day at STONE_VOLUME 0.03375 — matches standard
  rubble-walling labor constants (~0.8–1.0 m³/mason-day) and is consistent in kind with medieval
  walling paid by the perch (Knoop & Jones; Stafford/Lincoln accounts). **The verb is LAID:**
  dressing ran 0.5–4 stones/day at Guédelon ("each stone took two days to cut and dress," later
  "four stones in one day" — archaeology.org, Sep/Oct 2025). A chip or card that said "cut and
  laid N stones" would be off by an order of magnitude; dressing stays absorbed upstream in the
  quarry-side yield until a dressing step earns its own rate.
- **Laborers 3–5 m³/day** reads as loose-earth MOVING — mildly generous vs Holden's medieval-tools
  dig-in-place estimate (~0.42 m³/hr, a fatigue-honest day ≈ 2.4 m³; Herefordshire HTT), well below
  the navvy ceiling (9–19 m³/shift). Defensible as-is; beds.ts already prices in-situ rock
  separately and lower. Don't "tune it down for realism."

## 4. The timber way — the attestation boundary (PARTLY, with a hard line)

- **Genuinely medieval:** laid timber CAUSEWAYS as roads — the Norman corduroy road at Ely (1071,
  vs Hereward the Wake), the Berlin plank causeway (dendro-dated 1238). Sledges hauled over greased
  timber baulks are the experimentally supported heavy-stone technique (40-ton block, ~130 people,
  1:20 slope — 1997 NOVA/Stonehenge experiment).
- **NOT medieval:** guided-rail wagonways and anything conveyor-shaped — earliest firm examples are
  the Reisszug funicular (described 1515), De Re Metallica's mine hunds (1556), Keswick c. 1568.
  No pre-1500 evidence. Free log rollers over open ground are scholarship-doubted even for
  megaliths (no gouge traces).
- **The honest shape shipped:** the SLEDGE (the best-documented medieval overland device) RIDES a
  laid timber way. The mechanic delivers the boss's function (rapid transfer on a pre-planned
  path); the fiction never says rollers, rails, or conveyor.
- Bonus note for the roadmap, not this arc: history's first haul lever is WATER (Purbeck moved by
  sea from Wareham/Swanage wharves) — if the world ever grows a navigable reach, that's the arc.
