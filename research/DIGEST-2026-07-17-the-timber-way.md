# DIGEST — THE TIMBER WAY (haulage, causeways, carters) — 2026-07-17

*Verification pass for [PROPOSAL-THE-TIMBER-WAY-2026-07-17.md](../proposals/PROPOSAL-THE-TIMBER-WAY-2026-07-17.md).
Era target ~1200s England (Durham). Every claim carries its source and a confidence mark. Three
findings REFRAMED the design rather than merely sizing it — they are marked ★.*

---

## ★ 1. "Speed multiplier" is the wrong axis — the gain is THROUGHPUT, not pace

**VERIFIED.** Loaded and unloaded humans walk at nearly the same speed: the gross metabolic cost
curve is U-shaped with its minimum at **~1.3 m/s for *all* loading conditions**; preferred speed
1.24 ± 0.17 m/s. Carrying 30% of body mass raises metabolic rate **+47 ± 17%** — load costs
**energy, not pace**.
— Griffin, Roberts & Kram, *J. Appl. Physiol.* (2003).
<https://journals.physiology.org/doi/full/10.1152/japplphysiol.00944.2002>

**Consequence for the design.** A causeway cannot make a hand *walk* faster; modelled as literal
m/s the honest ceiling is ~1.2–1.5×. Modelled as **throughput** (m³·m moved per hand-day) a 3–6×
gain is fully defensible. **So the sim's carrier constant is a throughput budget, not a load × a
pace** — one labelled constant in m³·m/hand-day, which also sidesteps the unverifiable per-trip
load (§2). The player's visible payoff is *fewer hands on the road and more at the wall*, which is
true, legible, and exactly the boss's outcome ("the bricks reach their destination quicker").

## ★ 2. The multiplier belongs on the GROUND CROSSED, not on the causeway

Three independent lines converge on **~3× typical**, but all three agree the number is a property
of *what the road replaces*:

| Ground crossed | Defensible multiplier | Basis |
|---|---:|---|
| Firm, dry, hard ground | **1.0–1.2×** | Baker T8 (50/50 lb·ton⁻¹); Mairs (69 vs 57) |
| Ordinary earth | **~3×** | Baker T8 midpoints 3.1×; Mairs wet sod 3.0× |
| Mud / soft / wet / plowed | **4–7×** | Mairs plowed 4.4×; Baker T8 worst/best 6.7× |
| Marsh / fen | **effectively infinite** | impassable without a way (Ely, §4) |

**(a) Rolling — the standard engineering table. VERIFIED** (public-domain primary read). Ira O.
Baker, *A Treatise on Roads and Pavements* (Wiley, 1914), Table 8, tractive resistance lb/ton:
**plank road 30–50**; **earth road, ordinary 50–200**; sand 100–200; gravel 50–100; macadam 20–100.
→ plank vs earth **3.1× at midpoints**, 6.7× worst-earth vs best-plank, **1.0× if the earth is dry
and hard**. <https://archive.org/details/treatiseonroadsp00bake>

**(b) Rolling — a *measured* experiment. VERIFIED** (arithmetically self-checking: the printed
averages 130/148/186 reproduce from the reconstructed columns as 129.9/147.7/185.6). T.I. Mairs,
Missouri Agricultural Experiment Station Bulletin 52 (1902), iron-tired farm wagon ~1¾ t — close to
medieval cart technology. 50-inch wheels, lb/ton: macadam 57 · **earth dry+hard 69 (1.2×)** · 1"
sticky mud 101 · dry sod 132 · **wet spongy sod 173 (3.0×)** · **plowed dry cloddy 252 (4.4×)**.
*Bonus:* small wheels are punished far worse off-road (+23% on macadam vs **+48%** on plowed) — a
prepared way makes poor equipment much less punishing.

**(c) Sliding — the greased slipway. VERIFIED** (primary read in full). Richards & Whitby, "The
Engineering of Stonehenge," *Proc. British Academy* 92 (1997), 231–256:
μ = **0.05** for proprietary grease on 200 mm timber rails, **~0.1 for tallow** (the historical
lubricant) — p. 237/239. A 40 t stone + 1 t oak sledge needed just over **2 t of pull on the level**;
tallow instead of grease cost **+70 people** (130 → 200). Bare-ground comparator: a **1.5 t stone on
a simple sledge across level smooth grass took 32 haulers** (Atkinson 1956, cited p. 235).
<https://www.thebritishacademy.ac.uk/documents/3921/92p231.pdf>
→ tallow-greased timber (0.1) vs bare drag (0.3–0.6) = **3–6×**. The design's 3× is **conservative**.

> **★ HONEST COMPLICATION — it is the GREASE, not the timber.** Fall et al., "Sliding Friction on
> Wet and Dry Sand," *Phys. Rev. Lett.* 112, 175502 (2014): **wood-on-wood dynamic friction is
> μd = 0.25–0.7** (citing Blau, *Tribol. Int.* 34, 585 (2001)), while wet sand optimises at ~0.3 —
> so dragging over damp sand is *about as easy as over ungreased sleepers*. An **ungreased plank way
> is not a 3× improvement over decent ground.** <https://pure.uva.nl/ws/files/2365399/160672_Sliding_Friction_on_Wet_and_Dry_Sand.pdf>

**Two mechanisms the design was conflating, now separated:**
- **(A) Causeway over soft ground = BEARING CAPACITY** — it stops you sinking. Ely, Berlin, the
  Sweet Track. The gain is huge and *ground-dependent*. **This is what we built.**
- **(B) Greased slipway = LUBRICATION** — for heavy sledged blocks; the gain comes from tallow and
  works on any firm base. **Not built — see the follow-on below.**

## 3. Carters ARE a distinct trade in the 1200s — the job is honest

**VERIFIED** (read in full). Richard Sharpe, "Common Carriers in Medieval England: Evidence from
Oxford Archives," Oxford ORA:
<https://ora.ox.ac.uk/objects/uuid:53bbd65d-9c0c-489e-b4b6-431465840d10>
- Payments ***pro cariagio*** appear in accounts **from as early as the twelfth century** (p. 6).
- **"There were many carts available for hire in King John's time"** (1199–1216) — Farmer, in
  *Agrarian History of England and Wales* III p. 349, on the **Pipe Roll of the Bishopric of
  Winchester, 1210–11** (ed. Holt, 1964). *Dead-on the era.*
- Decisive: **Pipe Roll 17 Henry II (1170–71) p. 88** — ***"v caretis et iiij carectariis ad opus
  Regis in exercitu Hybernie"***: five carts and **four CARTERS** engaged for the king's work, for
  an extended period, not a single delivery.
- **Use the right word:** a **CARTER** (owns/hires cart + horses + labour for bulk loads) is
  attested 12th c. onward and is correct for 1200s Durham. A **COMMON CARRIER** (regular route,
  part-loads, many clients) is only from **~1449** — wrong for the era, keep it out.
- Carriage was also a **customary labour service owed by tenants** (Postles, *J. Transport History*
  3rd ser. 5 (1984), 1–15) — haulage could be *owed*, not hired. Relevant to the granary-as-
  government thesis.
- Troyes carters were recruited from villages at the route's **mid-point** and worked in **stages**,
  resting at home mid-way (Weller, on Piétresson de Saint-Aubin) — a route-stage mechanic falls
  straight out of this.

→ **`JobSkill += 'carter'` is attested, not invented.** The Carter surname has real substrate.

## 4. Loads, carts, and the causeway's cost

**Wheelbarrow — VERIFIED, and it lands precisely on the era.** First appearance in medieval Europe
**between 1170 and 1250**; four mentions in English/French sources **1172–1222** (M.J.T. Lewis, "The
Origins of the Wheelbarrow," *Technology and Culture* 35:3 (1994), 453–75, pp. 453–55, 463).
Earliest solid archival attestation: **1222, wheelbarrows purchased for the king's works at Dover**
(Andrea Matthies, "The Medieval Wheelbarrow," *T&C* 32:2 (1991), 356–64, p. 357). First depiction:
Matthew Paris, *Vitae duorum Offarum*, 1250.
**CAVEAT (VERIFIED):** the wheelbarrow **remained a rarity until the 15th century** (Lewis p. 456)
and was confined to England, France and the Low Countries.
**MYTH FLAG (VERIFIED as myth):** the often-repeated Chartres stained-glass wheelbarrow "soon after
1200" **does not exist** — the nearest thing is a *handbarrow* (Lewis p. 463). Discard it.
→ For Durham ~1200s the barrow is *just* attested and in exactly our project class (royal works),
but it should be **rare and not the stone-mover**. The sledge fiction holds.

**Cart capacity — VERIFIED, converging from two directions: ~1 tonne.** The "load"/"fother"/*carrus*
is in use by the 13th c.; the load of lead ≈ 2,100 merchant pounds ≈ **1,016 kg** (*Tractatus de
Ponderibus et Mensuris*, Edward I). Troyes cathedral fabric accounts, Tonnerre quarries: average
load **~15 medieval cu ft ≈ "a little more than one ton"**.
> **⚠ DISAGREEMENT — DO NOT USE GIMPEL.** *The Medieval Machine* p. 32 claims Troyes carters drove
> horse pairs pulling 2,500 kg wagons with 2,500 kg of stone. **Unfootnoted and demolished** by
> Weller: it traces via Hubert, *Les Routes du Moyen Age* pp. 39–40, which misapplies Piétresson de
> Saint-Aubin — an article spanning 1188–1588 and dominated by 16th-c. data. Verdict: **medieval
> carters were not hauling much more than a ton.** Gimpel's figure circulates widely on the web; it
> is bad. (Weller is self-published but densely footnoted to checkable primaries: PARTLY VERIFIED,
> and far more trustworthy than Gimpel.) <https://www.humanist.de/rome/rts/load.html>

**Draught power — PARTLY VERIFIED:** traction ≈ **10% of an animal's body weight** (horse 15–20%);
**no heavy draught breeds before the 17th–18th c.** — there were no big hauling horses in the
Middle Ages. **Ox→horse raised hauling speed from ~1½–2 mph to 3–4 mph** — a 12th–13th-c. English
development (VERIFIED: Langdon & Claridge, "Transport in Medieval England," *History Compass* 9/11
(2011), 864–75, p. 866).

**Working day — PARTLY VERIFIED, and later than the target.** York Minster masons' ordinances of
**1370** (Knoop & Jones, *The Mediaeval Mason*): winter daylight-to-dark, 1 h dinner, 15 min
drinking; summer sunrise to 30 min before sunset, 1 h dinner, 30 min drinking, 30 min sleeping →
**~8¾ h winter, ~12¼ h summer**; winter wages cut, commonly by a sixth.
*Honesty:* consistent across secondaries all citing Knoop & Jones, but the indexed full text is a
**dead host** and the ordinances are **1370, not the 1200s**. For 1200s Durham the safe statement is
"sunrise to sunset, less breaks."

**Causeway cost — UNVERIFIED for the era.** No attested medieval per-metre timber volume or labour
was found. The **Sweet Track** (VERIFIED but **Neolithic, 3807/3806 BC** — ~4,900 years early):
~1,800 m, ~200,000 kg of timber → **~111 kg/m**; Coles: once materials were on site **ten men could
have assembled it in one day**.
> **⚠ Read that labour figure carefully.** "Ten men in one day" is **assembly only** — it excludes
> felling, splitting, working and hauling ~200 tonnes of timber to a marsh, which is where
> essentially all the real cost is. **Do not model causeway cost from that number.** (We did not:
> `WAY_TIMBER_PER_M` / `WAY_WORK_PER_M` are labelled game choices.)

**Ely 1071 — PARTLY VERIFIED, and the sources are literary.** *Liber Eliensis* + *Gesta Herewardi*:
a **mile-long timber causeway** at Aldreth; willow bundles woven into mats, overlaid with earth and
timber; the *Gesta* adds timbers over inflated sheepskins to float it. **It SANK under the weight of
armour and horses, drowning many.** The *Gesta* is 12th-c. and often fantastical. **No cost or
labour figures — its value is the FAILURE MODE, not a number.**

## 6. Follow-ons this digest opens (noted, NOT built)

- **★ TALLOW AS A CONSUMABLE.** The friction evidence says the timber is nearly inert without
  grease (ungreased wood-on-wood 0.25–0.7 ≈ bare ground). Tallow gating the causeway's bonus is
  both a good mechanic and the *scientifically correct* one — and tallow is animal fat, which ties
  straight to the herds the BACKLOG already reserves. The strongest single follow-on here.
- **CAUSEWAYS FAIL.** Ely's sank under armour and horses and drowned men — attested in both
  chronicles. A drawn causeway over marsh that can *sink* under an over-heavy load is historically
  real and a genuine decision.
- **ROUTE STAGES.** Troyes carters worked in stages from the route's mid-point, resting at home —
  a real mechanic for long hauls.
- **CARRIAGE AS CUSTOMARY SERVICE.** Haulage owed by tenants rather than hired (Postles) — fits the
  granary-as-government civic thesis.
- **THE OX→HORSE STEP.** A verified 12th–13th-c. English doubling of hauling speed (1½–2 → 3–4 mph):
  a real, dated, era-appropriate upgrade the pasture's draft horses could unlock.
